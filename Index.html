A Decade of Spending on AI

Visualizing the Massive Increase in Government Speading on Autonomous Surviallance and Weapons Systems

By Craig Bonder

The last decade has seen the unprecedented rise of a few tech-focused startups, taking an increasingly large share of the government's budget. Palantir, Anduril, and a few others are leading the charge in the U.S. government's pivot to AI & autonomous systems in a wide array of applications. Palantir and Kratos focus on AI-enhanced data collection & management systems, while Anduril, Skydio, and AeroVironment produce autonomous weapon platforms like the Switchblade 300, which fits in a backpack and launches from a tube before "loitering" over a target area, waiting to strike.

While nowhere near the size of the defense "Primes" (Lockheed Martin, RTX, etc.), Palantir, AeroVironment , Anduril, Kratos, and Skydio in particular have seen explosive growth since the start of the 2022 Russian invasion of Ukraine. These five companies have nearly doubled the size of their government contracts since the start of the war, from $700 Million in 2022 to over $2.3 Billion in 2025. Investors are also expecting continued growth with the U.S.'s return to war in the Middle East.

The Ukrainian front has shown the world's defense leaders that a massive shift in battlefield dynamics is underway. What started with Predator drones in Iraq and Afghanistan has evolved into cheap, often backpack-sized drones carrying grenades, jammers, or other payloads, deploying straight from a platoon level. This dynamic has shifted the Department of Defense's view of AI and autonomous weaponry, with Pete Hegseth championing the DoD's new AI Initiative to build an "AI First" combat force.

While these technologies are deployed and celebrated on the warfront, many eventually make their way home. The Department of Homeland Security paid Palantir $145M USD in 2022 for a case management system, often called ELITE, which scrapes government data for potential targets. Similarly, Customs and Border Protection paid Anduril $362M USD for autonomous surveillance towers along the U.S.–Mexico border, called "Sentry", over 300 of which monitor the southern border currently.

The conflict with Iran has also sparked increased usage of lightweight AI & autonomous systems on both sides. Iran spearheaded its counterattack on American military bases and allied energy infrastructure with its Shahed drones , which it also sold to Russia for frontline use in Ukraine. Mirroring the conflict in Europe, Reuters reports that five Ukrainian drone interceptor units have been deployed to the Middle East in the last week, presumably to help U.S. forces implement more modern drone defense techniques.
